About
Development of a Philippine Indigenous Instrument Sounds Database

The Philippines already had a rich and unique musical tradition long before the Spanish colonizers set foot in the country. Given that the country is an archipelago, there is diversity in terms of music traditions and instruments used. Such instruments as flat gongs, bamboo buzzers, clappers, quill-shaped percussion tubes, and brass Jew’s harps are often seen in the north. Meanwhile, bossed gongs, ring flutes, log drums, xylophones, single-stringed violins, and suspended beams are commonly used in the south. These musical instruments are used in various rituals, festivities, and other activities. Today, a small minority of Filipinos continues to practice these traditions.

The aim of this project is to bridge the gap between traditions and the current generation. A database of Philippine music instrument sounds was created. Variation in the instruments’ pitch, dynamics and playing style were considered. The sound database is available through this online portal. The online portal also contain metadata and related multimedia for each recorded instrument. Thus, the project not only aims to preserve music traditions, but also to promote creative reuse, educate, and promote opportunities for research and collaboration.

The project, funded by the Department of Science and Technology (DOST), is a multi-agency research and development effort of the Electrical and Electronics Engineering Institute, University of the Philippines Diliman, UP Center for Ethnomusicology, and Advanced Science and Technology Institute.
